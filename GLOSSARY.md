# Glossary

Working definitions of the core concepts we publish about. These are meant as
accessible reference points, not exhaustive academic definitions — follow the
sources for depth. Spotted an inaccuracy?
[Tell us](https://github.com/the-qthe-standard/.github/issues/new/choose).

### Safety-I
The traditional view: safety is the absence of accidents, achieved by finding
and eliminating what goes wrong. Focuses on failures, error counts, and
compliance.

### Safety-II
The complementary view (Erik Hollnagel): safety is the *presence* of things
going right. Focuses on why work usually succeeds, and how people adapt to make
it succeed under varying conditions.

### Work-as-Imagined (WAI)
How work is assumed to happen — the procedures, plans, and rules written by
people who are not doing the work in the moment.

### Work-as-Done (WAD)
How work actually happens — the real-world adaptations people make to get the
job done under real constraints. The gap between WAI and WAD is where both risk
and resilience live.

### Resilience Engineering
A discipline studying how systems sustain operations under expected and
unexpected conditions — the abilities to *respond*, *monitor*, *learn*, and
*anticipate* (Hollnagel's four cornerstones).

### Human Factors
The scientific study of the interaction between people, systems, and
environments, used to design work that fits human capabilities and limits —
rather than blaming humans for poor design.

### Human & Organizational Performance (HOP)
An operating philosophy built on principles such as: error is normal, blame
fixes nothing, context drives behavior, learning is vital, and how leaders
respond matters. A practical bridge from Safety-II thinking to daily management.

### Just Culture
An accountability framework that distinguishes human error, at-risk behavior,
and reckless behavior — responding to each appropriately so people stay willing
to report and learn, rather than hide problems.

### Psychological Safety
The shared belief that it is safe to speak up — to ask questions, raise
concerns, admit mistakes, or challenge decisions — without fear of punishment or
humiliation. A precondition for surfacing Work-as-Done.

### Drift / Normalization of Deviance
The gradual, often invisible movement of normal practice away from the safe
baseline, as shortcuts that "worked last time" become routine — until conditions
align for failure.
